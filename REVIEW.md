# I Tried This Writing Analysis Tool and It Actually Made Me Think

**By Sarah Chen | The Verge | February 2026**

---

I've reviewed dozens of "AI writing tools" over the past three years. Most promise to make you a better writer. What they deliver is autocomplete with a thesaurus. So when Sentense landed in my inbox with the tagline "discover your writing voice," I prepared my usual skepticism.

Ninety seconds later, I was staring at my screen, genuinely surprised.

## What It Is

Sentense is a free web app that analyzes your writing and tells you what kind of writer you are. You paste in some text (or write fresh), complete a short "stretch challenge," and get a profile card with your writing style and what it calls your "epistemic stance"—basically, how certain or uncertain your prose sounds.

No account required. No AI-generated suggestions. No grammar fixes. Just... a mirror.

## What It Gets Right

**The profiles feel earned.** I'm apparently "The Voice in the Room"—conversational, warm, immediate. The description nailed something I've never quite articulated about my own work: *"You write like you talk—or rather, like the best version of how you talk."* That landed.

But here's what elevated it beyond a personality quiz: the stance detection. Sentense told me I write with a "balanced" epistemic stance, mixing certainty and qualification. Then it showed me exactly which sentences were doing what. I could *see* my hedges. I could see where I commit and where I waffle.

**The stretch challenge is clever.** After analyzing my initial text, it asked me to write one sentence "without hedging"—to state something with full conviction. This tiny exercise taught me more about my defaults than any writing course I've taken.

**The literary ancestors feature is a nice touch.** Being told I write in the tradition of David Sedaris and Nora Ephron felt flattering (probably by design), but the reading list it generated actually included books I hadn't read. That's useful.

## What's Missing

Let's be clear about what Sentense *isn't*: it's not a writing improvement tool. It's a writing awareness tool. The distinction matters.

There's no feedback loop. I can see my patterns, but Sentense doesn't track whether I'm changing them over time. The "deep analysis" screen shows metrics like average sentence length and question frequency, but without historical comparison, it's a snapshot, not a journey.

**The social features feel bolted on.** There's a "save with Facebook/Instagram/LinkedIn" section that doesn't actually connect to anything—it just pretends to. I get that this is a beta, but the mock functionality is jarring. Either ship the feature or remove it.

**The "compare with friend" feature is half-baked.** You generate a code, share it, and your friend enters theirs to see how your profiles differ. Cute concept, but the comparison is superficial—just profile names side by side with a one-liner like "Opposites attract: you strip down, they build up." I wanted more. How do our sentence structures differ? Where do our stances diverge?

**No mobile optimization.** The app works on my phone, but the experience is cramped. For something that asks you to *write*, the input areas feel like an afterthought on smaller screens.

## The Uncomfortable Truth

Here's what I keep thinking about: Sentense doesn't try to change my writing. It doesn't suggest "stronger" verbs or flag passive voice. It just... observes.

And that observation stung a little.

I discovered I hedge more than I realized. My "balanced" stance might actually be indecision dressed up as nuance. The stretch challenge—write one sentence without qualifiers—was harder than it should have been.

That discomfort is the product working.

## Who Is This For?

Sentense is for writers who are curious about their own patterns—and honest enough to look. It's not for people who want to "improve their SEO" or "write faster with AI." It's for the person who wonders why their emails always sound apologetic, or why their blog posts never quite land.

It's also genuinely fun at parties. I made three colleagues try it during a slow Friday afternoon, and we spent an hour comparing profiles and arguing about whether "The Careful Scholar" was a compliment or a burn.

## The Business Model Question

Here's my concern: I don't see how this makes money.

Right now, Sentense is free. No premium tier. No ads. No data collection beyond anonymous analytics. The privacy notice explicitly states that your writing never leaves your browser.

That's admirable, but it's also unsustainable. Either Sentense becomes a freemium product (with what features behind the paywall?), or it gets acquired, or it dies. I've seen too many beloved tools follow this trajectory.

The team should be thinking about this now. A "pro" tier with historical tracking, team comparisons, or integration with writing tools like Notion or Google Docs would be natural extensions. I'd pay $5/month for that.

## The Verdict

Sentense is a small, thoughtful product that does one thing well: it shows you how you write. Not how you *should* write. Not how AI thinks you should write. Just... how you actually do.

In an ecosystem bloated with tools promising to make everyone sound the same, there's something subversive about a product that simply holds up a mirror.

It's not finished. The social features need work, the mobile experience is weak, and the lack of a business model makes me nervous about its longevity. But the core insight—that writers benefit from seeing their patterns, not just fixing their grammar—feels right.

Try it. Paste in an old email, a blog draft, something you wrote when you weren't trying to impress anyone. See what it tells you.

You might not like what you find. That's the point.
